Yes, and possibly more.* (In roughly the order of likelihood in the next 20 years)

  1. Puerto Rico has skirted at the edge of being either a state or an independent country for the past decade. The majority of the people dont care for the territory status as it is now so one of those two may happen in the foreseeable future.
  2. Then there is Washington D.C. that has been trying to become a state for a long time.
  3. In northern California and southern Oregon, a growing movement is advocating carving out a state of Jefferson from the two portions of the other states.
  4. The Virgin Islands are in a similar position as Puerto Rico in that a large number of their people are unhappy with territory status and want to be either independent or a state.
  5. Marshall Islands, Micronesia, and Palau have an unusual relationship in a compact of free association with the United States. A growing movement on the islands is calling for statehood.
  6. American Samoa has a movement for statehood too.
  7. Guam has been trying to upgrade their status to something more like Puerto Rico. The last proposal was rejected by Congress. As a result, a growing number of people now support statehood instead (albeit along with others that desire independence.)
  8. As Cuba becomes more open and more Americans are allowed inside, there is a possibility that the people may wish to join the United States. Cuba was once a territory of the United States and shares a common cultural history with southern Florida.
  9. New York City has enough people to break from New York and take some of New Jersey with it to become another state. New York City is very different from the rest of the state politically, culturally, and economically and the idea has been discussed before.
  10. Texas has a clause in its constitution that would allow it break itself into five states with a simple vote in the state legislature.
  11. There have been proposals of creating a virtual state made up of Americans living abroad. They are already treated as such in election primaries.
  12. Northern panhandle of Florida has had brief considerations of breaking away from Florida to become another state.
  13. Some people of the upper part of Michigan have expressed a desire to secede from the rest of the state (and possibly taking parts of Wisconsin) to become a state of Superior. It was seriously considered in 1897, again in 1962, and at various points in the 70s.
  14. If Mexico tips towards becoming more unstable, it is conceivable that parts close to the border could break away and join the United States. Areas like Tijuana could be states one day.
  15. The Chinese consider Taiwan a rogue province, but the island has always been friendlier with the United States than with China. The U.S. has been reluctant to anger China with pushing for more independence for Taiwan but a new administration may change that. Could they become a state?
  16. The Philippines have long been referred to an unofficial American state. They have faced a recent history of political turmoil and a downturned economy. Could they possibly look towards statehood as a better option?
  17. Liberia is a country on the African continent colonized by the descendants of freed slaves. Could their shared history eventually lead them to joining the United States?

    * Keep in mind that Article IV Section 3 of The Constitution says New states may be admitted by the Congress into this union; but no new states shall be formed or erected within the jurisdiction of any other state; nor any state be formed by the junction of two or more states, or parts of states, without the consent of the legislatures of the states concerned as well as of the Congress.

Fascinating question. Although there are a couple of candidates, its possible that the current 50 we have now might be the final number for a long, long time, although I suspect its a close call.

One thing to consider is that the current make up of the US Senate is hotly contested. Making DC into a state would add 2 Senators to the US Senate who would be the most liberal in the entire Senate, permanently mainly for the simple reason that DC is 100% urban area. Peurto Rico also likely to be permanently and strongly liberal, as is Hawaii, and I would think the GOP would be against admitting either.

There are cultural reasons why the current makeup of 50 states might stay as is

  1. Theres an understandable bias in favor of the 48 continental states because almost every country in the world does things that way, geographically speaking. Non-contiguous home soil is very hard to defend. For the US to consider a state to be home soil despite being over 1,000 miles away from any other state (Hawaii) is exceptional, almost unknown.
  2. There is a strong cultural bias, I think, in favor of places where most residents speak English as their first language . So, even though PR is almost bilingual, it is seen as very different.
  3. Hawaii is an exceptional case, and I believe it wouldnt be a state today were it not for World War II. Most Hawaiian residents today would not bother to campaign for statehood, but after WWII, the sacrifice made by its people (especially Japanese nationals who volunteered to fight in the European Theater) created a feeling that Hawaii had earned statehood. And now that Hawaii has been a state for more than half a century, the economic advantages of staying in the Union are too good to want to break up the status quo. Even the presidency of Barack Obama probably helped a bit, because to leave the Union would almost amount to making Birtherism correct, retroactively and I suspect the residents want to stand behind Obama.
  4. Alaska was also an unusual case. Although not originally colonized by the US, Alaksa had become the new home of many from the USA, thanks partly to Seward and partly to the Yukon Gold Rush. There is a country between Alaska and the State of Washington et al., but that in between country is Canada, considered extremely friendly.

Again, the case of Hawaii is fascinating. (Read James Micheners great novel about the state.) In addition to WWII, which I think was decisive for statehood, I think the great influx of New England whalers and missionaries (whose children stayed and became wealthy sugar and pineapple planters) in the 19th Century was also critical; there were enough of those groups to widely spread the English language and once again, the adoption of English as the first language does more than anything else to make a territory look like one of us. (Yes, I understand that there is a growing number of Hispanics in our own Southwest.)

So DC and Puerto Rico (PR) are still interesting cases, but its possible the USA might remain at the magic number of 50 for the foreseeable future. Beyond that, its hard to see.
